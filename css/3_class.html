the haunted library

The haunted library stood at the edge of the old town, a crumbling relic of forgotten knowledge and whispered legends. Its towering structure, once a proud center of learning, now loomed under a veil of neglect, its windows shattered like broken eyes and its wooden doors swollen with age. Locals avoided it, spinning tales of strange sounds, flickering lights, and shadows that moved without reason. Yet, for those brave—or foolish—enough to step inside, the library held an irresistible pull. Dust hung thick in the air like a permanent fog, coating every shelf and surface, while the scent of decaying paper and damp wood seeped into the lungs. Books were scattered everywhere, some stacked neatly as if recently touched, others torn open with pages missing, as though something—or someone—had been searching. The silence inside was not peaceful but oppressive, a heavy stillness that made every footstep echo louder than it should. Occasionally, a faint whisper could be heard, too soft to understand yet too deliberate to ignore. Many believed the whispers were the voices of past readers, trapped within the pages they once studied. The deeper one ventured, the more the atmosphere seemed to shift, as though the building itself were alive, watching and waiting. Stories spoke of a librarian who had vanished decades ago, last seen cataloging a mysterious collection of ancient texts. Some claimed he never left, that his spirit still roamed the aisles, promise of hidden knowledge. But those who entered with confidence rarely left unchanged. Many emerged pale and shaken, unable to explain what they had experienced, while others refused to speak of it at all. A few never returned, their disappearance adding to the library’s growing legend. It was said that the building fed on curiosity, luring in those who sought answers and trapping them within its endless maze of shelves. As night fell, the library became even more sinister, its darkness swallowing any trace of the outside world. The wind would howl through its broken windows, creating eerie melodies that sounded almost like distant chanting. Lights would flicker deep within its halls, despite there being no electricity, and shadows would stretch unnaturally along the walls. Those who dared to approach after sunset often felt an overwhelming sense of dread, as if the library itself were warning them to stay away. Yet, for some, that warning only made the mystery more enticing, drawing them closer to the secrets hidden within its decaying walls.

the birth of loard hanumanji

The birth of Lord Hanumanji is one of the most sacred and fascinating stories in Hindu mythology, filled with divine purpose, celestial blessings, and extraordinary events. Hanumanji is believed to be an incarnation of Lord Shiva, born to serve Lord Rama and assist in the destruction of evil. His mother, Anjana, was a celestial nymph (apsara) who had been cursed to be born on Earth. She performed intense penance and prayed to Lord Shiva, seeking liberation from her curse. Pleased with her devotion, Lord Shiva granted her a boon that he himself would be born as her son. Around the same time, King Dasharatha of Ayodhya was performing the sacred Putrakameshti Yagna to be blessed with sons. The divine pudding (prasadam) from the yagna was meant for his queens, but through a divine intervention carried by the wind god Vayu, a portion of that sacred offering reached Anjana. With the blessings of Lord Shiva and the divine essence of the yagna, Anjana conceived a child who was destined to become one of the greatest devotees and warriors in history. Thus, Hanumanji was born with immense strength, wisdom, and divine energy, symbolizing courage, devotion, and selfless service. From the moment of his birth, it was clear that he was no ordinary child, but a powerful being sent to Earth for a higher purpose. As a child, Hanumanji displayed extraordinary powers and boundless energy that often led him into playful yet remarkable adventures. One of the most famous incidents from his childhood is when he mistook the rising sun for a ripe fruit and leaped into the sky to grab it. With incredible speed and strength, he soared across the heavens, astonishing the gods and celestial beings. Seeing this, Lord Indra, the king of the gods, became alarmed and struck Hanumanji with his thunderbolt (Vajra), causing him to fall back to Earth and injuring his chin. This is said to be the reason he came to be known as “Hanuman,” derived from the word “Hanu,” meaning jaw. The wind god Vayu, who is considered Hanumanji’s spiritual father, was deeply angered by this act and withdrew air from the universe, causing all living beings to suffer. To pacify Vayu and restore balance, the gods blessed Hanumanji with numerous divine powers, including immense strength, the ability to change size, invincibility, and protection from various weapons. However, due to his mischievous nature, sages later placed a mild curse on him, causing him to forget his powers until reminded by others. This ensured that his strength would always be used wisely and only when needed. These early events not only highlight Hanumanji’s divine origins but also set the stage for his future role as a devoted servant of Lord Rama. The true purpose of Hanumanji’s birth became evident during the time of Lord Rama, when he played a crucial role in the epic Ramayana. As a devoted follower of Rama, Hanumanji exemplified the highest form of bhakti (devotion), dedicating his life entirely to serving his lord. His strength, intelligence, and unwavering faith made him an indispensable ally in Rama’s quest to rescue Sita from the demon king Ravana. Hanumanji’s leap across the ocean to Lanka, his fearless encounter with Ravana, and his act of setting Lanka ablaze are legendary feats that continue to inspire millions. Despite possessing immense power, he remained humble and always attributed his abilities to Lord Rama’s grace. His devotion was so pure that he is often regarded as the embodiment of selfless service and loyalty. Even after the victory of Rama, Hanumanji chose to remain on Earth, continuing to bless and protect devotees who remember him with faith. It is believed that he is immortal (Chiranjivi) and still present wherever the name of Lord Rama is chanted. The story of Hanumanji’s birth and life teaches valuable lessons about devotion, humility, courage, and righteousness. His divine journey reminds us that true strength lies not in power alone, but in using that power for the service of others and in unwavering dedication to truth and dharma.

the mention's curse

The story of the cursed mention began in a forgotten village where people believed that names held power—real power. It was said that speaking certain names aloud could summon things that should never be called. Most dismissed it as superstition, until a young man named Raghav stumbled upon an old, torn manuscript in an abandoned house at the edge of the village. The pages were filled with strange symbols and repeated mentions of a single name, scratched over and over again as if written in desperation. At the bottom of one page, a warning was barely visible: “Do not speak the name after midnight.” Curious and skeptical, Raghav laughed it off. That night, sitting alone in his room, he read the name aloud. At first, nothing happened. The silence remained undisturbed, almost mocking him. But then, the air grew heavy. His lamp flickered violently, and a faint whisper echoed from somewhere behind him. It sounded like someone repeating the same name—slowly, painfully. Raghav turned around, but there was no one there. The whisper grew louder, closer, until it felt like it was coming from inside his own head. From that night on, things began to change. Every time someone mentioned that name—whether in thought, speech, or even accidentally reading it—the presence grew stronger. Raghav started seeing shadows in places where light should exist. Mirrors reflected movements that didn’t match his own. At exactly midnight, the whisper returned, calling him, urging him to speak the name again. He tried to ignore it, but the more he resisted, the more aggressive it became. One night, unable to bear it anymore, he shouted the name loudly, as if to confront whatever was haunting him. The room went completely silent. Then came a sound—a slow, dragging noise, like something crawling across the floor. From the corner of his room, a dark figure began to form, not fully visible, but unmistakably there. Its shape twisted unnaturally, as if it didn’t belong to this world. When it spoke, its voice was a distorted echo of Raghav’s own. “You called me again.” The next morning, Raghav was gone. No signs of struggle, no clues—just the manuscript lying open on his bed. But something had changed. The name that had once filled the pages was gone, replaced by a new line written in fresh ink: “Now, you are the mention.” Days later, villagers began hearing whispers again, this time in a voice that sounded eerily familiar. Anyone who tried to recall or repeat the story found themselves unknowingly speaking the name. And with each mention, the curse spread. It didn’t need rituals, it didn’t need belief—it only needed to be spoken. Even now, people say the curse still exists, hidden in stories, waiting to be read aloud. And if you ever feel a sudden urge to repeat a word you don’t remember learning… stop immediately. Because once you speak it, you don’t just summon the curse—you become part of it.

the story of loard rama's exile

The story of Lord Rama’s exile is one of the most emotional and powerful episodes from the epic Ramayana, reflecting sacrifice, duty, and unwavering commitment to righteousness. Lord Rama, the eldest son of King Dasharatha of Ayodhya, was the rightful heir to the throne. The entire kingdom was filled with joy as preparations began for his coronation. However, fate had a different plan. Queen Kaikeyi, influenced by her maid Manthara, was reminded of two boons that King Dasharatha had once granted her. Driven by fear and insecurity, Kaikeyi demanded that her son Bharata be crowned king and that Rama be exiled to the forest for fourteen years. Bound by his promise, King Dasharatha was heartbroken but helpless. When Rama learned of this, he accepted the decision without hesitation, showing his deep respect for his father’s word and his commitment to dharma. His calm acceptance stunned everyone, as he chose duty over personal desire, setting an example of ideal character. As Rama prepared to leave for the forest, his wife Sita insisted on accompanying him, declaring that her place was always by his side, whether in palace or wilderness. Despite Rama’s attempts to persuade her to stay back and live in comfort, Sita remained firm in her decision. Similarly, his devoted younger brother Lakshmana could not bear the thought of being separated from Rama and chose to join them in exile. Together, the three left the grand city of Ayodhya, dressed in simple garments, leaving behind royal luxuries. The citizens of Ayodhya were overwhelmed with grief and followed them for miles, unwilling to let their beloved prince go. Rama, with compassion and wisdom, convinced them to return, reminding them of their duties and responsibilities. Meanwhile, King Dasharatha, unable to bear the separation from his beloved son, passed away in sorrow. Bharata, who was away at the time, returned to find the kingdom in mourning and was devastated to learn about the events. Refusing to accept the throne gained through injustice, he went to the forest to bring Rama back, but Rama refused, choosing to honor his father’s promise. Bharata then placed Rama’s sandals on the throne and ruled Ayodhya as his representative, waiting faithfully for his return. During their exile, Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana lived in forests like Chitrakoot and Panchavati, facing numerous hardships and dangers. Despite the challenges, Rama remained calm and righteous, protecting sages and defeating demons that troubled the region. Their life in the forest was simple yet filled with purpose. However, this period also set the stage for the great events that would follow, including the abduction of Sita by Ravana, the king of Lanka, which led to the epic battle described in the Ramayana. The exile was not merely a punishment but a journey that tested Rama’s strength, patience, and adherence to dharma. It highlighted the values of sacrifice, loyalty, and truth. Even in the face of injustice and hardship, Rama never wavered from the path of righteousness. His exile became a symbol of moral strength and ideal conduct, teaching generations the importance of keeping one’s word and fulfilling one’s duties. The story continues to inspire millions, reminding us that true greatness lies not in power or position, but in character and integrity.
